🫀 The Cardiac Story — Plain English

The most important finding across your entire health record is your CT coronary angiogram from May 2024. This scan looked inside your actual coronary arteries using contrast dye and found two key things: a Coronary Artery Calcium (CAC) score of 523, placing you at the 75th percentile for a man your age, and — more significantly — a mixed plaque in your LAD (left anterior descending artery) that is causing a moderate stenosis of approximately 30%. The LAD is sometimes called the "widow maker" artery because it supplies a large territory of the heart muscle. A 30% narrowing is not an emergency — it does not require a stent or bypass — but it is a real lesion that demands respect, medication optimization, and regular monitoring.

The reassuring counterpoint: your October 2025 nuclear stress test was genuinely good news. You exercised to 92% of your predicted maximum heart rate, your heart showed no reversible perfusion defects (meaning no areas went without blood flow during stress), and your ejection fraction — the measure of how powerfully your heart pumps — is 66%, which is excellent. POST-SURGERYTarget: <0.1Post-prostatectomy nadirPSA should drop to undetectable (<0.1 ng/mL) within 6–8 weeks of surgery. This is the most important post-op test.
Post-prostatectomy PSA definition of recurrence: A PSA that rises to ≥0.2 ng/mL on two consecutive measurements after reaching an undetectable nadir is considered biochemical recurrence (BCR). BCR does not necessarily mean metastatic disease — it may indicate residual local disease or micrometastatic disease that can be managed with salvage radiation. The earlier it is caught, the more treatable it is. This is why your PSA should be checked every 3–6 months for the first 2–3 years, then every 6–12 months if stable.
🎯 Post-Prostatectomy Surveillance & Action Plan
❗ Critical Surveillance Protocol
❗PSA every 3 months for years 1–2. Must reach undetectable (<0.1 ng/mL) as the nadir. Any detectable PSA that then rises to 0.2+ on two draws = biochemical recurrence requiring immediate oncology action.
❗PSA every 6 months in years 3–5 if nadir is achieved and stable. Then annually thereafter if consistently undetectable.
⚡Given pT3a staging — discuss with your urologist whether adjuvant radiation therapy (ART) to the prostate bed is appropriate. Some guidelines recommend consideration of ART for pT3a with focal EPE even with negative margins, particularly if PSA does not reach undetectable or rises early.
⚡If biochemical recurrence occurs — salvage radiation to the prostate bed is most effective when PSA is still below 0.5 ng/mL. Do not wait. Early salvage radiation has cure rates of 50–70% for pT3a recurrence.
📋 Quality of Life & Recovery
🏃Pelvic floor exercises (Kegel exercises) — begin immediately post-catheter removal. The most evidence-based intervention for urinary continence recovery after prostatectomy. Do 3 sets of 10–15 contractions daily.
💊Erectile function recovery — penile rehabilitation (PDE5 inhibitors — sildenafil or tadalafil at low daily doses) started early post-surgery significantly improves long-term erectile function recovery. Discuss with urologist within the first month post-op.
🧬Testosterone therapy consideration — historically deferred after prostate cancer. However, emerging evidence (and current AUA guidelines) suggest TRT may be considered in prostatectomy patients with undetectable PSA and appropriate follow-up period (typically 1–2 years of stable undetectable PSA). Given your significant testosterone decline (599 → 399), this is worth discussing with your oncologist at the 1-year surveillance mark.
🍎Diet after prostate cancer: Mediterranean diet, reduced red meat, increased cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, Brussels sprouts contain sulforaphane — studied for prostate cancer recurrence reduction), and maintaining a healthy weight all have supporting evidence for reducing recurrence risk.
🏋️Exercise reduces recurrence risk — vigorous exercise (3+ hrs/week) is associated with 61% lower prostate cancer-specific mortality in post-treatment patients. This is one of the strongest lifestyle-cancer links in the literature. Your existing exercise routine is directly protective.
